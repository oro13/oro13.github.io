Quickly assessing a Market Place Business

Notes on Lenny Rachitskey's (ex-AirBnB) interview on the [Two-Sided podcast](https://www.sharetribe.com/twosided/)

Context: Lenny Rachitskey was an early employee of AirBnB and credited for playing a key role in growing operations to the scale we see today. When he left the company, many people started seeking him out for advice on their own company. Only being able to pull from his own experience at one company, he decided to reach out to key players at 17 (at the time of this interview) top market place tech companies that are well known today. These include Uber, Lyft, Thumbtack, Task Rabbit, Etsy, Patreon, and so on. He offers this advice with the disclaimer that there are no static, fool proof methods for succesful market platforms.

## Overview

### 1. Start with something people actually want

is this a good product? is it solving a problem for people? Is this something people purchase once for cheap or is it a reoccurring or high value transaction?

### 2. Don't assume what HAS worked WILL work

Just because it worked for Company X doesn't mean it will work for you. Growth channels change; costs change; opportunities for building gravity for your platform may be specific to certain policy or incentives specific to that market. You can't just do XYZ and assume it'll work.

### 3. Focus on building a "white hot center'' in your market place

Put your energy and resources to build a "white hot center," something thats working really well, even at a tiny scale before focusing on growth strategies. This stage may include strategies that don't scale well beyond a certain location or number of participants. 

For example, when Uber was just beginning, it paid its drivers a salary to ensure there would be enough supply for rides for a user opening the app at any given time of day. There is an art to overcoming this "chicken and egg" phase of the market platform, where most projects end up dying before the potential buyers and sellers can even find the app and make use of its coordination. More on this topic in the Kick Starting a Market Place section below.

## Strategy 1: Quickly assessing a Market Place Business

### 1. Is this a good business in general? 

In other words: *NO BUSINESS CAN BE SAVED IF IT'S OFFERING SOMETHING NO ONE WANTS*

What is the product-market fit?

- Is this traction & early amazing feedback?

- Is retention high (of supply and demand) 

- **most important**: Is the market big enough to become a business?

Why this team? 

- why are they the right ones to build it, their insights and experiences

Why now? 

- what's changed about the world, technology or the market that allows for this now? 

What strategic advantage do you have?

- What "moats" do you have? 
- What's gonna stop someone from coming and just squashing you and winning?
- How strong is the business model in general 
  - for example, software has a great business model, it's why it's "eating" the world
    - it's easy to scale, high margins of profit, relatively low capital needed

Are you looking ahead?

- Is there are growth strategy? (often taken for granted)

###  2. Is this a good market place business?

*What are the attributes that will make this work as a **market** business*

Is this something both sides of the markets want? (buyers and sellers)

-  unlike a regular business, you need product market fit on both sides, make supply and demand both happy.
  - demand side - is this better than the alternatives (non marketplace company)
  - supply side - is this meaningful income, can this be enough money to be worth my time, 
    - people at home making some extra money business is matching up both sides for a win-win
    - e.g. a airbnb for storage company has to find the right balance of **affordability** for buyers and 'worth my time' factor for the sellers, encouraging enough people to clear up some space in their garage for $50 is there enough people who would do that? is there enough money in it to incentivize people to clear up space. its are occurring payment, don't have to bring people back but they're already set to pay for multiple months 
    - high frequency and high order value (you need at least one but ideally both) people purchase it often or spend a lot of money on it report on top 100 market place companies, not a single company is both, more than half were neither (there's opportunity if you cant make these, like you need an efficient way to acquire supply, not a huge margin for each user, need a big market for it to turn a profit as well) is supply highly fragmented? (if i want this, can I go get it myself or is it hard to find)
    - e.g. airbnb, its hard to find a home in a foreign place vs airline, easy to find relationship between supply and demand 'non-monogamous'? (when I use your site am I done forever?)
      - eg uber you use once vs finding a nanny. For a nanny, I find them once and then its done. There's a higher risk of disintermediation and platform leakage increases in markets for finding a 'nanny'. 

- is there a reason to stay on the platform to avoid disintermediation, a reason to stay on the site, a reason to use the site rather than booking directly
  - e.g. an insurance policy are you paying through the platform vs paying directly?
  - e.g. pay airbnb or pay the host directly when i arrive 
  - This is arguably why Craigslist is slowly getting disrupted 
  - is this better than a none market place alternative?
  - is there a better way to provide the service with a better customer experience?
    - there's nothing magic about a market place other than being a more efficient way to run a business
